JHR Blog Writing Guidance — Relationship‑First, SEO‑Smart (Feb 2026)
Purpose of This Document
This is the single source of truth for anyone writing blog content for JHR.
Our blogs are not marketing assets in the traditional sense. They are relationship tools. They exist to reduce uncertainty, build trust, and sound like the calm, capable teammate our clients experience in real life.
Write every post as if it will be read by someone managing multiple vendors, tight timelines, and internal expectations — because it will be.

1. Start With the ICPs — Not Keywords
We do not write blogs for traffic.
We write for specific decision‑makers under pressure, and we let search engines catch up.
Across all four ICPs, the shared reality is:
They are smart, busy, and responsible for outcomes
They are not browsing for inspiration
They are trying to reduce uncertainty
That changes everything about how we write.
How ICPs Show Up in Blog Strategy

Therefore, our blogs must read like:
A producer explaining how things really work
A local partner removing unknowns
A calm guide — not a thought leader

2. Brand Voice: Warm, Calm, Operational
Our voice is not “SEO‑optimized content voice.”
It is JHR voice, applied with SEO discipline.
We write like people who have been in the room before.
Good JHR Blog Tone
Professional, but conversational
Familiar and friendly — like one of the team
Empathetic without emotional fluff
Confident without marketing language
Clear, scannable, and calm
Not JHR Blog Tone
“We’re passionate about…”
“In today’s fast‑paced event landscape…”
Over‑clever metaphors
Generic marketing or trend commentary
If it sounds like content marketing, it’s wrong.
If it sounds like a capable teammate, it’s right.

3. Relationship‑Based Writing (This Is the Core)
JHR is a relationship‑based company. Our writing must reflect that.
When someone reads a JHR blog, it should feel like:
A trusted teammate explaining nuance
A partner who understands the pressure
Someone genuinely invested in their success
We are always providing value first — never posturing, never selling.
What “Familiar and Friendly” Actually Means
This IS the JHR voice:
“Here’s what we usually see when planners come into Nashville.”
“This is one of those details that doesn’t matter — until it really does.”
“If this is your first show here, this part tends to surprise people.”
“Our goal is to take this off your plate so you don’t have to think about it again.”
This is NOT the JHR voice:
Corporate thought leadership language
Over‑polished brand copy
Performative enthusiasm
Warmth comes from understanding, not hype.

4. Writing With Emotional Intelligence
Assume the reader is:
Managing multiple vendors
Accountable to internal stakeholders
Carrying reputational pressure
Short on time
Write accordingly.
Required EQ Signals in Every Post
Validation: “If this feels overwhelming, that’s normal.”
Alignment: “Here’s how this usually plays out on real shows.”
Support: “This is something we handle for teams all the time.”
Partnership: “We look at this the same way you do.”
We do not dramatize stress.
We calmly absorb it.

5. Blog Structure for SEO & AI (GEO)
Search engines and AI models reward the same thing we already believe in:
Clarity beats cleverness.
Required Blog Anatomy
1. Answer‑First Opening (40–60 words)
This is snippet bait for:
Google featured snippets
ChatGPT / Perplexity answers
Internal forwarding
It must:
Directly answer a real planner question
Sound human and helpful
Establish calm authority immediately
2. Human Headers (Not Keyword Dumps)
We do not write:
“Best Nashville Event Photography Services 2026”
We write:
“What Actually Makes a Nashville Media Partner Easy to Work With”
AI understands meaning, not keyword stuffing.
3. Entity Nesting (Critical for GEO)
Naturally reference:
Venues
Neighborhoods
Event types
Systems (not services)
Example:
“At the Music City Center, timing matters more than lighting.”
This builds topical authority and local trust.
4. Insider / Operator Insight (Citation Bait)
Every strong post includes at least one:
Process insight
Local nuance
Execution reality
Not secrets for hype — details planners recognize as real.

6. Writing for AI Without Sounding Like AI
AI engines reward content that is:
Clearly structured
Specific, not vague
Grounded in lived experience
Reusable in chunks
Which means:
Short paragraphs
Clear H2s with direct answers underneath
Lists that explain why, not just what
We are not optimizing for AI.
We are writing operator‑grade clarity, which AI prefers by default.

7. The Strategic Through‑Line (Non‑Negotiable)
Every blog should quietly reinforce:
The client is the hero
Uncertainty is the problem
JHR is the calm guide
Systems > heroics
Assets must be usable
This should be felt — not announced.

8. Litmus Test Before Publishing
Before a post goes live, ask:
Does this reduce uncertainty?
Does this sound like someone who’s been on site?
Does this feel generous with knowledge?
Would a planner forward this internally without editing the tone?
Does this feel easy to read under pressure?
If yes → publish.
If not → revise.
